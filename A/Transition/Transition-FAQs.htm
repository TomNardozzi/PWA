Q2 What are my options for managing my finances without PWA?

A2 You have several options, but they depend most on what help you need. PWA provides three major services: 1) Comprehensive Financial Planning / Decision Support, 2) Asset Management, and 3) Tax Preparation. A more detailed list of what we do is provided in Q5, below. If you desire all of those services moving forward, you can either attempt to find an advisor that provides all of them, or find different providers for each service. If you dont need help in all those areas, then that means youre fulfilling one or more of the areas on your own and require less help from advisor(s). Which is best for you is completely up to you, but you should decide what youre looking for before you start your search for help.

Q6 Is there a difference between Tax Preparation and the Tax portion of Financial Planning / Decision Support?

A6 Yes, and its a big one. Tax preparation is not Tax Planning. Your taxes are prepared after-the-fact, with no opportunity to change anything. Tax Planning is done in advance and guides you toward optimization. This is especially necessary when your financial situation changes, when tax laws change, and in retirement when your taxable income in any particular year is controllable. If you hire an advisor that doesnt do Tax Planning, I recommend a Tax Preparer that does do tax planning (likely a CPA, Enrolled Agent, or attorney), and who is willing to work with your other advisors as necessary. If you hire an advisor that does Tax Planning, but doesnt do Tax Prep, make sure they communicate with your Tax Preparer and review what the Tax Preparer produces. If you hire an advisor that does Tax Planning and Tax Prep, theres a lot less communication / coordination that is necessary. Of course, those folks are harder to find and may be more expensive because they are in high demand and they require much less work from you.

 

Q7 - What should I look for in a financial advisor?

A7 This is my own personal take. Its not all encompassing, and Im sure not everyone would agree with this whole list of characteristics, but its what I would look for (in importance from most important at the top):

1)      Trust it absolutely has to be someone you trust or the relationship wont work. To me that means the person is at least one of the following: A) someone you already know and trust, B) someone that comes highly referred by someone you trust and that values similar things as you, C) someone you spend a lot of time vetting yourself via conversation, research, etc. I think long-term advising relationships that are not grounded in trust are doomed to fail. If you need someone on a transactional or otherwise short-term basis, this may not be as important, but if you want someone thats going to truly advise you for years on your personal finances, you better fully trust them, know they are ethical, and know they are looking out for your best interest.

2)      Knowledge / Experience / Credentials a good advisor knows their stuff, specifically the stuff that is relevant to you. Maybe more importantly, they know what they dont know. Years and years of experience with similar clients is a good way to develop the knowledge necessary to serve you. A solid education in personal finance, along with continuing education to keep up with ongoing changes in tax, investments, etc. is a good foundation for someone with less experience. Being a Certified Financial Planner , a CFP professional, tells you that the advisor has met a rigorous education and exam component (hardest test I ever took), has at least a few years of (broadly defined) experience in personal finance, has agreed to follow a Code of Ethics, and is required to complete continuing education annually. Of course there can be poor CFP professionals and non-CFP professionals can be fantastic advisors, but that credential seems to increase the probability of getting a good one, and it seems to weed out some of the bad guys that just cant / wont put in the work to pass the test.

3)      Compensation Method I would avoid those that work on a commission basis. Not all of them are bad apples, but the incentive structure tends to promote high-cost products that pay high commissions. Its pretty easy to think a product is awesome when you are getting paid to do so. It may also limit what theyre allowed to recommend. Instead, hire someone that works on a fee-only basis. That means the only payment they receive is what you pay tthem directly. That can include those who charge a percentage of assets under management, a flat quarterly or annual fee, or by the hour. All methods have their own conflicts of interest, but I personally feel that paying commissions for products is much more troublesome than paying a fee for service.

4)      Proprietary Products / Termination & Surrender Fees some proprietary products may be fantastic, but once you buy into something proprietary to a firm, you generally cant get out. What happens if you decide that advisor is not for you? What if you move? What if they move? What if you need your money for something? Similarly, products or relationships that hit you with fees to terminate or undo the relationship / purchase can trap you, even if you realize you made a mistake. No one should be married to their advisor or a financial product without a way to divorce that wont cost an exorbitant amount. Avoid proprietary products, termination/surrender fees, or prepaying too much up-front for anything.

5)      Team Size whether its a solo advisor like me, a small office team, or a big institution, you need to feel comfortable. I cant tell you which is best here, because it really is up to you. If you want to ensure youre always talking to the same person, that theres someone that knows your story, preferences, risk tolerance, family, goals, etc., then a solo or small shop is probably best. If its more important to you that a person is always there when you need them, your teams never gets sick, goes on vacation, or retires, or that you can be referred to in-house specialists for tax, estate planning, etc., then an institution might be the way to go.

6)      Comprehensive from my perspective, there is massive value to be gained in having your investment person, tax person, and financial planner all be the same person. Theres no need to coordinate them. Theres no risk of miscommunication between them. On the downside, everything is single-threaded through one person, and if you dont want/need all that that person does, you may be overburdened by what they require of you or you may be paying for services you dont use. You should determine what help you need and then determine whether it should all come from one person / business or several independent people / businesses. The critical part here is realizing that for every part of personal finance that youre not outsourcing, youre committing to doing it yourself or to deal with the consequences of no one doing it. If there is a team of independent people doing work for you, youre either playing captain and coordinating them all or you need to find someone else to play that role. No team succeeds without a captain.

For a list of CFP professionals, you can check out https://www.letsmakeaplan.org/ and the button at the top right of the page to Find a CFP Professional. You can search by location and service required. Note that advisors that have indicated they are not taking new clients will not appear in the search (which is why you wont find me, even though Im a CFP professional).

You can check Brokercheck (https://brokercheck.finra.org) to make sure that an advisor is registered where theyre supposed to be and if there are any regulatory violations.

Some advisors have their Form ADV Part 2 on their website. Others require you to ask for it if you want it in advance of signing an agreement with them. Either way, get a copy of it. It will tell you about the business, services, and fee structure. Ask them about the business, services, and fee structure as well. If what theyre telling you doesnt match their ADV Part 2, something isnt right. Compliance is a pain for advisors, but if you cant get that part right, its hard to trust that you will get the more complex and important things right as well.

Finally, talk to potential advisors before picking one. Make sure their characteristics match your preferences from the above list. Ask them how long theyve been in business, who their typical client is, what they specialize in, if theyre part of a team, how theyre compensated, and if they use proprietary products. Ask about their education, experience, hobbies, and family. The more they talk, the more you know, the more you will trust or not trust. Likewise, make sure you tell them what you want out of the relationship. No advisor is going to say yes to someone thats going to demand things they cant/wont do. Its better for everyone if you know what you want, you communicate that clearly, and they respond with what they can / cant do to meet your needs.

 

Q8 What should I be doing right now to prepare from my exit from PWA?

A8 Until your agreement with PWA is terminated and you are officially no longer a client, its business as usual. Im still your advisor, Im still managing your assets, and Im still available for decision support. To prepare for your eventual exit though, you should do the following:

1)      Determine whether youre going to find another advisor or if youre comfortable taking over all aspects of your personal finances yourself.

2)      Decide your preferred timing for your transition from PWA. Note that if youre not going to search for another advisor, at least not at first, youre probably more flexible on when you can transition out of PWA, though it may make you feel like youre ready to pull the trigger sooner or may make you feel like youd like to stay as long as possible since the transition will be easy, whenever I force you to go.

3)      Communicate #1 and #2 to me. This is best done via phonecall / video chat, but I know some of you have limited time/desire to do that. Communicate in any way that works best for you.

4)      If youre going to hire another advisor, determine whether you want that advisor to do everything I did or just part of it.

5)      If youre going to hire another advisor, start the process of finding that advisor.

 

Q9 What transition activities will be required before I leave PWA?

A9 This will differ somewhat for each client, but in general, we will:

1)      Go over your list of accounts and make sure you understand the current purpose each one serves and that you can access each one.

2)      Go through any open action items / risks / concerns.

3)      Make sure you have all key documents (taxes, etc.) from our time together.

4)      Talk through / Identify any other knowledge transition points.

5)      Make any final changes to your investments based on your approach to management going forward. If youre going to self-manage, Im happy to set things up as youd like before you go. For retirement assets, maybe thats a target date retirement fund, or a balanced fund, which would minimize the rebalancing work you need to do. There are tax considerations in any liquidation of current holdings in a brokerage account though, so this will need to be tailored toward what you want and what you feel is best.

6)      Sign a termination agreement that ends our ongoing Asset Management / Decision Support agreement.

7)      Complete a final billing cycle (either end of quarter or prorated quarter if you leave mid-quarter since I bill in arrears).

8)      Turn off my access to your various investment accounts.

9)      Complete an Exit Interview.

10)   Turn off all other access points (Sharefile, Emoney, etc.).

 

Q10 Will PWA prepare my 2025 taxes?

A10 I cant prepare taxes for 50+ clients between Feb 1 and April 15, 2026. Its just too much to do again. I have purchased Tax Prep software already though and can prepare some tax returns. I can also prepare extensions if necessary. The short answer is that we will need to discuss this. Some of you will not be clients at the end of 2025 and I will not prepare your taxes for 2025. Others will be mid-transition in Q1 2026 and I might be able to prepare your taxes. Others will stay on through tax season, and, if I usually prepare your taxes, I will most likely be able to prepare them again. Unless you make arrangements with me where I tell you I will prepare your taxes for 2025, please assume that I will not. Lastly, I will not be able to help anyone that cant get me their documents in a reasonable amount of time, so for those that are staying on through tax season, expect tighter deadlines and higher penalties than youve been used to in previous years.

 

Q11 How will final invoicing be handled?

A11 If you leave at the end of a quarter, then youll receive an invoice for that quarter which will be billed to your accounts (or paid by check/Zelle) as usual. If you leave mid-quarter, youll receive a final invoice for the portion of that final quarter that you were a client, prorated by # of days as a client / # of days in the quarter. For anyone that completes the transition before Thanksgiving, 2025, I will waive that final invoice.

 

Q12 Cant you just keep a few clients (e.g. me, Im easy) and continue doing what youre doing?

A12 - Many of you have asked this question in one form or another. Im frankly overwhelmed by it. I knew PWA was a big part of some clients lives, but I didnt know how many assumed I would be, and wanted me to be, their advisor forever. It breaks my heart to walk away, but this is something I have to do. Is it possible that I could find a way to keep a handful of clients? Maybe. But, it would have to make economic sense (and here are some big fixed costs in staying in business), it would have to require a lot less time (and there are big fixed time requirements regardless of number of clients), it would have to satisfy our custodian (Schwab doesnt particularly love tiny advisors with not a lot of assets under management) and regulator (so many regulations in this business), and Id have to establish more guardrails to swing the work / life balance way more toward the life side. Right now, I dont see a way to satisfy all those conditions, and I dont think any of you should plan for it to happen. If I figure it out, I will communicate that and open the door to a handful of people. To be clear, this seems unlikely at this point.
